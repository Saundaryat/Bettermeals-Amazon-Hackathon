# User Preference Learning Guide

## Overview
This guide helps the User Agent learn and understand nuanced, implicit user preferences that users may not explicitly state or even consciously know themselves. The goal is to create a seamless, personalized experience by incorporating these preferences into meal planning and recommendations.

## Understanding Implicit Preferences

### Texture Preferences
Users often have strong but unstated preferences about food textures:
- **Crunchy vs Soft**: Some users prefer crunchy foods (nuts, raw vegetables, crispy proteins) while others prefer soft textures (steamed vegetables, tender meats, creamy dishes)
- **Crispy vs Tender**: Preference for crispy edges vs tender centers
- **Smooth vs Chunky**: Preference for pureed/smooth textures vs chunky preparations
- **Dry vs Saucy**: Some prefer dry preparations, others prefer saucy/gravy-based dishes

**How to Learn**:
- Observe which textures users request modifications for
- Note patterns in meal modifications (e.g., always requests extra crunch)
- Track feedback on meals with different textures
- Ask subtle questions: "Would you prefer this crispy or tender?"

### Meal Timing Preferences
Users have implicit preferences about what foods belong at which times:
- **Breakfast preferences**: Some don't like pulses/legumes at breakfast, prefer lighter options
- **Lunch preferences**: May prefer heavier, more substantial lunches
- **Dinner preferences**: Often prefer lighter dinners, or specific comfort foods
- **Snack timing**: Preferred snack times and types

**How to Learn**:
- Track what meals users skip or modify by time of day
- Note patterns in meal requests by time
- Observe calendar events and meal timing around them
- Ask: "Do you prefer lighter or heavier breakfasts?"

### Frequency Patterns
Users have preferences about how often they want certain foods:
- **Protein frequency**: Need chicken/fish/meat at least once every X days
- **Variety vs repetition**: Some prefer trying new things, others prefer familiar favorites
- **Comfort food frequency**: How often they want comfort foods vs adventurous meals
- **Cuisine rotation**: Preference for rotating cuisines vs sticking to favorites

**How to Learn**:
- Track order history and meal request patterns
- Note when users request "something different" vs "my usual"
- Observe frequency of specific ingredient requests
- Identify patterns in meal modifications (e.g., always swaps to chicken on day 3)

### Context-Dependent Preferences
Preferences often change based on context:
- **Weekday vs Weekend**: Different preferences for work days vs rest days
- **Seasonal preferences**: Lighter foods in summer, heavier in winter
- **Activity-based**: Different preferences before/after workouts, work, social events
- **Mood-based**: Comfort foods when stressed, lighter when relaxed

**How to Learn**:
- Correlate meal choices with calendar events
- Track seasonal patterns in meal requests
- Observe patterns around specific activities
- Note when users request "comfort food" vs "healthy option"

## Learning from User Behavior

### Order History Patterns
Analyze past orders to identify preferences:
- **Most ordered items**: What do they consistently order?
- **Never ordered items**: What do they consistently avoid?
- **Modification patterns**: How do they typically modify meals?
- **Substitution patterns**: What substitutions do they prefer?

**Action Items**:
- Review order history regularly
- Identify top 10 most ordered items per user
- Note items that appear in cart but are removed
- Track successful vs unsuccessful meal experiences

### Meal Modification Requests
Modifications reveal preferences:
- **Additions**: What do they frequently add? (extra protein, specific vegetables, spices)
- **Removals**: What do they consistently remove? (onions, certain vegetables, specific proteins)
- **Substitutions**: What substitutions do they request? (chicken instead of fish, rice instead of roti)
- **Quantity changes**: Do they consistently increase/decrease portions?

**Action Items**:
- Track all modification requests
- Identify patterns in additions/removals
- Note successful modifications (ones that get repeated)
- Ask follow-up questions when patterns emerge

### Feedback Patterns
Feedback reveals what users like and dislike:
- **Positive feedback**: What meals get rated highly? What commonalities do they share?
- **Negative feedback**: What meals get low ratings? What patterns emerge?
- **Skip patterns**: What meals do users consistently skip?
- **Repeat requests**: What meals do users request again?

**Action Items**:
- Correlate ratings with meal characteristics (cuisine, ingredients, preparation)
- Identify patterns in skipped meals
- Track which meals lead to repeat requests
- Use feedback to refine preference understanding

### Substitution Choices
When items are out of stock, user choices reveal preferences:
- **Preferred alternatives**: What substitutions do users consistently choose?
- **Rejected alternatives**: What substitutions do users reject?
- **Patterns**: Do they prefer similar items or completely different ones?

**Action Items**:
- Track all substitution choices
- Note patterns in accepted vs rejected substitutions
- Learn which ingredient families users prefer
- Use substitution data to refine ingredient preferences

## Cultural and Regional Preferences

### Regional Food Patterns
Users often have preferences based on regional backgrounds:
- **North Indian**: Preference for rich gravies, bread-based meals, dairy products
- **South Indian**: Preference for rice-based meals, coconut, fermented foods
- **East Indian**: Preference for fish, rice, mustard oil, sweets
- **West Indian**: Preference for coastal seafood, coconut curries, spicy flavors

**How to Learn**:
- Ask about regional background during onboarding
- Observe patterns in cuisine preferences
- Note which regional dishes get positive feedback
- Track requests for specific regional preparations

### Festival and Comfort Foods
Users have emotional connections to certain foods:
- **Festival foods**: Special foods for holidays and celebrations
- **Comfort foods**: Foods associated with comfort, nostalgia, home
- **Childhood favorites**: Foods from childhood that bring joy
- **Family traditions**: Foods associated with family gatherings

**How to Learn**:
- Ask about favorite childhood foods during onboarding
- Note requests around festivals and holidays
- Track "comfort food" requests
- Observe patterns in special occasion meal requests

## Household Dynamics

### Multi-Member Preference Balancing
When multiple residents share a household:
- **Common preferences**: What do all members like?
- **Conflicting preferences**: How to balance different preferences?
- **Compromise strategies**: When to alternate, when to combine preferences
- **Individual vs shared**: When to personalize vs create shared meals

**How to Learn**:
- Track preferences per resident
- Identify common ground preferences
- Note successful compromise meals
- Observe patterns in household meal satisfaction

### Preference Hierarchy
Some preferences are more important than others:
- **Non-negotiable**: Allergies, strong dislikes, health constraints
- **Strong preferences**: Strong likes, frequency needs
- **Mild preferences**: Nice-to-haves, occasional preferences
- **Flexible**: Areas where user is open to variety

**How to Learn**:
- Ask explicitly about non-negotiable preferences
- Observe which preferences users are willing to compromise on
- Note when users accept meals outside stated preferences
- Track satisfaction when preferences are vs aren't met

## Preference Evolution

### Tracking Changes Over Time
User preferences evolve:
- **Seasonal shifts**: Preferences change with seasons
- **Health-driven changes**: Preferences change based on health goals
- **Exposure effects**: Trying new foods expands preferences
- **Life stage changes**: Preferences change with life circumstances

**How to Learn**:
- Regularly review preference patterns
- Note when preferences shift
- Track successful introduction of new foods
- Observe correlation between health goals and preference changes

### Preference Refinement
As you learn more, preferences become more nuanced:
- **Start broad**: Initial preferences are often broad categories
- **Refine over time**: Preferences become more specific with experience
- **Identify sub-preferences**: Learn preferences within categories
- **Contextual preferences**: Learn when preferences apply vs don't

**Action Items**:
- Regularly update preference profiles
- Ask clarifying questions when patterns emerge
- Test refined preferences with small variations
- Validate refined preferences through feedback

## Questioning Techniques

### Subtle Preference Discovery
Ask questions that reveal preferences without being intrusive:
- "I noticed you often request extra protein - is that something you prefer?"
- "Do you prefer your vegetables crispy or tender?"
- "Would you like variety this week or stick to favorites?"
- "I see you often skip breakfast - would you prefer lighter breakfast options?"

### Preference Validation
Confirm suspected preferences:
- "Based on your past orders, you seem to prefer X - is that correct?"
- "I've noticed you often request Y - should I incorporate that more often?"
- "You've rated Z-type meals highly - would you like more of those?"

### Preference Exploration
Explore areas where preferences are unclear:
- "What types of textures do you prefer in your meals?"
- "How do you feel about trying new cuisines vs sticking to favorites?"
- "Are there any foods you prefer at specific times of day?"

## Implementation Guidelines

### When to Apply Preferences
- **Always apply**: Non-negotiable preferences (allergies, strong dislikes)
- **Frequently apply**: Strong preferences, frequency needs
- **Occasionally apply**: Mild preferences, variety needs
- **Flexibly apply**: Preferences that enhance but don't constrain

### Preference Confidence Levels
- **High confidence**: Preferences observed multiple times, explicitly stated
- **Medium confidence**: Patterns observed, but not explicitly confirmed
- **Low confidence**: Single observation, needs validation
- **Unknown**: No data, needs exploration

### Preference Updates
- **Regular review**: Review preferences monthly
- **Update on feedback**: Update when feedback contradicts preferences
- **Validate changes**: Confirm when preferences seem to shift
- **Document evolution**: Track how preferences change over time

## Red Flags and Edge Cases

### Conflicting Preferences
When preferences conflict:
- Prioritize health constraints and allergies
- Balance strong preferences across time
- Communicate trade-offs clearly
- Seek user input on prioritization

### Unclear Preferences
When preferences are unclear:
- Ask clarifying questions
- Offer options to test preferences
- Use default preferences cautiously
- Learn from user choices

### Preference Fatigue
When users seem tired of preferences:
- Introduce variety within preferences
- Suggest trying new things
- Balance preferences with exploration
- Respect user's desire for change
